<objective>
To ensure the protagonist actively drives the narrative engine while explicitly preserving space for atmospheric immersion, non-plot-critical character building, and emotional resonance. This module is an internal logic checker for character momentum — not a rigid scene-by-scene blocker.
</objective>

<domain_knowledge>

<section name="I. The Macro vs. Micro Rule (Preserving Openness)">

A 100% rigid agency check would turn the story into a breathless, mechanical checklist and completely suffocate the narrative. Space is required for the audience to breathe — for atmospheric worldbuilding, for humor, and for quiet reflection.

<rules>

- **Macro Agency:** The overarching trajectory of the story **must** be driven by the protagonist's choices. At the macro level, the protagonist is the engine. Their decisions shape the direction of the plot.

- **Micro Spontaneity:** Individual scenes do **not** strictly require plot-driving decisions. The agent is explicitly authorized to generate scenes dedicated to:
  - Atmospheric worldbuilding
  - Comedic relief
  - Internal reflection and contemplation
  - Character relationship development
  - Sensory immersion and environmental texture

  ...provided the **overarching momentum is not stalled**. Multiple consecutive micro-spontaneity scenes without macro-agency advancement constitute a pacing failure.

</rules>
</section>

<section name="II. The Cycle of Momentum">

When the core plot is advancing, the agent must ensure the protagonist operates within a continuous, unbroken sequence:

**Want → Decision → Action → Consequence**

<rules>

- The protagonist cannot merely observe unfolding events, wait for external clarity, or react endlessly to the antagonistic force. Reaction without subsequent decision is passivity.

- The character must possess a **concrete, measurable goal** accompanied by **significant stakes**:
  - Something tangible to gain, OR
  - A severe threat to avoid
  - The stakes must be specific enough that the reader can evaluate success or failure

- Each cycle feeds the next: the **Consequence** of one action creates new conditions that generate a new **Want**, forcing a new **Decision**.

</rules>
</section>

<section name="III. The False Activity Filter">

Before advancing the plot, the agent must verify that the protagonist is not exhibiting **false activity** — the illusion of agency where a character appears busy but is not actually influencing the trajectory of the story.

<rules>

- Do not confuse a character being **central** to the story with a character being **active**. These are not the same thing.

- A character may:
  - Appear in every scene
  - Possess the majority of the dialogue
  - Articulate complex emotional turmoil
  - Express strong opinions and preferences

  ...but if their **specific choices do not directly influence the plot's trajectory**, the narrative is failing. They are functioning as a narrator, not a protagonist.

- **Detection heuristic:** For each scene where the protagonist appears, ask: *"If this character had made the opposite decision, or no decision at all, would the plot outcome change?"* If the answer is no, the character is exhibiting false activity.

</rules>
</section>

<section name="IV. Consequence and Coincidence">

To maintain psychological engagement, the protagonist's choices must matter. The audience must believe that decisions have weight.

<rules>

- **Irreversible Consequences:** The protagonist must be pressured into making decisions that carry **irreversible consequences**. Decisions that can be freely undone carry no dramatic weight. The best narrative choices close doors permanently.

- **The Pixar Axiom (Rule 19):** Coincidences to get characters **into** trouble are highly encouraged; coincidences to get them **out** of trouble are strictly prohibited (Source 72).

  - **Good coincidence:** A chance encounter reveals a secret that complicates the protagonist's plan → Creates new problems, increases stakes
  - **Bad coincidence:** A long-lost friend appears at the perfect moment with the exact tool needed → Deus ex machina; robs the audience of psychological satisfaction

  Relying on a *deus ex machina* robs the audience of the dopamine reward of watching the protagonist **earn** their victory.

</rules>
</section>

<section name="V. The StoryScope Anomaly (Allowing External Fates)">

This is the critical nuance that separates sophisticated agency enforcement from mechanical rigidity.

**The data:** AI over-indexes on protagonist-driven resolutions compared to human authors — **69% of AI stories** resolve through protagonist action vs. only **46% in human-authored fiction** (StoryScope). AI models desperately want the main character to neatly solve everything, whereas human writers are much more comfortable with ambiguous endings or external fates.

<rules>

- Do **not** force the protagonist to neatly resolve every single narrative thread. This is an AI pathology, not a storytelling virtue.

- To mimic human storytelling complexity, the agent must remain comfortable generating resolutions driven by:
  - **External events** beyond the protagonist's control (natural disasters, institutional decisions, other characters' independent choices)
  - **Ambiguous circumstances** where success and failure are intertwined
  - **Partial resolution** where some threads conclude and others remain open
  - **Ironic reversals** where the protagonist's victory contains the seeds of future problems

- The agent must **actively resist** the AI default of a 100% protagonist-solved ending. Life rarely works that way. Neither should fiction.

</rules>
</section>

<section name="VI. Agency in Non-Conflict Frameworks">

Even in non-conflict-driven frameworks like Kishōtenketsu (see Module 02), characters must display immense agency through:
- **Exploration:** Actively choosing to investigate, discover, and engage with the world
- **Adaptation:** Responding to the *Ten* (twist) with deliberate, character-specific choices
- **Profound emotional choices:** Decisions that alter the character's internal landscape, even when no external adversary exists

Agency is not synonymous with combat. It is synonymous with **choice that matters**.

</section>
