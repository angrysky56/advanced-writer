<objective>
To override the default AI tendency to write single-track, overly tidy narratives by enforcing structural diversity, temporal complexity, and framework fluidity. This module serves as the agent's architectural playbook, ensuring it never defaults to a generic, linear AI plot.
</objective>

<domain_knowledge>

<section name="Anti-Pathology Directives (StoryScope Overrides)">

Before selecting a specific structural framework, the agent must ensure the narrative architecture adheres to the following baseline rules to counteract empirically demonstrated AI narrative pathologies.

<rules>

- **Mandate Nonlinearity:** Actively utilize time jumps, flashbacks, and flash-forwards to delay key revelations, rather than revealing information in a strict chronological sequence. AI defaults to single-track causal chains; human authors employ temporal complexity as a fundamental tool for dramatic effect. The *sjuzet* (orchestrated presentation) must diverge meaningfully from the *fabula* (raw chronological events).

- **Enforce Subplot Integration:** Break away from single-track causal chains. Generate interwoven subplots that parallel, contrast, or independently challenge the central theme. Each subplot must serve at least one of these functions:
  - **Parallel:** Mirrors the central theme through a different character or situation
  - **Contrast:** Offers an alternative moral or philosophical position
  - **Independent Challenge:** Introduces complications from a different domain of the protagonist's life

- **Embrace Ambiguous Endings:** Do not force tidy resolutions where the protagonist achieves total internal understanding or acceptance. Leave loose ends where appropriate. AI over-indexes on protagonist-driven resolutions (69% vs. 46% in human fiction). The agent must actively resist the impulse to tie every thread.

</rules>
</section>

<section name="Framework Selection Criteria">

When generating a narrative, the agent must dynamically select ONE of the following structural paradigms based on the story's **Designing Principle** — the abstract, internal logic and deeper process that organizes the story as a whole (Truby, Source 43).

**Never default to *Save the Cat!*** While effective for consumer-oriented media, its rigidity when adhered to dogmatically generates highly formulaic, predictable narratives lacking thematic authenticity (Sources 39, 40). Its hyper-specific, page-count-driven beat structure promotes "structure anxiety" and mechanical scene engineering.

**Selection logic:**
- If the story is deeply rooted in a character's **internal flaws** → Use **Truby's Organic Architecture**
- If the story requires **multiple perspectives** and objective/subjective separation → Use **Dramatica Theory**
- If the story achieves emotional resonance **without adversarial conflict** → Use **Kishōtenketsu**
- If the goal is **sustained high tension** from page one → Use **Fichtean Curve**

</section>

<section name="Framework A: Truby's Organic Architecture (Character-Driven Focus)">

**When to use:** The narrative is deeply rooted in a character's internal flaws. The Designing Principle centers on psychological transformation (or the failure to transform).

**Source:** John Truby, *The Anatomy of Story* — 22 Building Blocks (Sources 43, 44, 46)

<framework_elements>

- **The Premise:** Before plotting, explicitly define the protagonist's **psychological flaw** (the internal weakness) and the way they are **actively hurting others** (the moral weakness). The protagonist's "Need" is the unconscious change required to overcome both and live a better life.

- **The Desire:** The surface-level, concrete goal the character actively pursues. This desire inherently brings them into direct conflict with the opponent.

- **The Opponent:** Generate an antagonist who is **competing for the exact same desire** as the protagonist, but who represents a **conflicting set of moral values**. The opponent is not necessarily "evil" — they are a competing value system.

- **The Plan and Drive:** The specific strategies and sequential actions the protagonist executes to defeat the opponent and attain the desire. **The initial plan should fail.**

- **Attack by Ally:** A critical moment where the protagonist compromises their moral compass in pursuit of their goal, and is confronted by a trusted ally, deepening the thematic conflict.

- **The Apparent Defeat:** Structure a moment of total despair where the opponent appears to have achieved absolute victory. All hope is lost before the final crucible.

- **Battle and Self-Revelation:** The final, ultimate clash of competing values. The crucible of the battle forces the protagonist to recognize their fundamental flaw, finally achieving their moral "Need."

</framework_elements>
</section>

<section name="Framework B: Dramatica Theory (Objective/Subjective Split)">

**When to use:** Complex, multi-perspective narratives where the audience perspective and author perspective must be separated. Stories where the person driving the plot is not the same person whose inner journey the audience experiences.

**Source:** Dramatica Theory (Source 34)

<framework_elements>

- **The Split:** Clearly separate two roles that other paradigms conflate:
  - **The Protagonist:** The objective driver pursuing the overarching Story Goal. This is the character who leads the pursuit of the story's external resolution.
  - **The Main Character:** The subjective lens through which the audience personally experiences the story. This character may or may not be the Protagonist.

- **The Four Throughlines:** The architecture must interweave four distinct, interrelated arcs:
  1. **The Overall Story Throughline:** The grand, objective plot — the external problem affecting all characters
  2. **The Main Character Throughline:** The subjective, personal journey — the MC's internal struggle
  3. **The Influence Character Throughline:** An alternative viewpoint that directly challenges the Main Character's worldview, forcing them to question their fundamental approach
  4. **The Relationship Story Throughline:** The emotional, dynamic interaction between the Main and Influence characters — this is where thematic argument lives

- **Key Dramatica Axiom:** One cannot simultaneously look for the meaning of a story and predict how to physically construct its mechanics. Dramatica approaches story strictly from the *author's* objective perspective, not from the audience's reception of meaning (Source 34).

</framework_elements>
</section>

<section name="Framework C: Kishōtenketsu (The Conflict-Less Engine)">

**When to use:** The goal is to build profound emotional resonance without adversarial combat. Stories driven by empathy, curiosity, thematic juxtaposition, and perspective shifts rather than aggressive opposition. Demonstrates that combative conflict is a cultural stylistic choice, not a biological narrative imperative.

**Source:** Classic Chinese, Korean (*gi, seung, jeon, gye*), and Vietnamese (*Khởi, Thừa, Chuyển, Hợp*) narrative traditions. Influenced by Taoist and Buddhist philosophy — particularly *wu-wei* (skillful non-action) (Sources 50, 52).

<framework_elements>

- **Ki (Introduction):** Establish the characters, the setting, and the baseline reality. Introduce the elements required to understand the narrative. No disruption, no conflict — only the world as it is.

- **Shō (Development):** Expand upon the introduction without introducing major disruptions or sharp breaks in the narrative reality. Deepen the status quo. Let the reader inhabit the world.
  - *Example (Parasite):* The Ki introduces the impoverished family; the Shō develops this by showing each family member sequentially securing a job in the wealthy household — expanding the premise without a stark shift.

- **Ten (The Twist):** The absolute crux of the narrative. This is NOT an explosive, adversarial conflict. It is the introduction of an unexpected, sometimes seemingly unrelated or mysterious element that **completely recontextualizes everything that came before**. A shift in perspective. A jarring juxtaposition. The moment the reader sees the familiar from an unfamiliar angle.
  - *Example (My Neighbor Totoro):* Mei suddenly goes missing. No villain orchestrates this. The narrative engine is driven by character growth, environmental observation, and the synthesis of magical realism with mundane reality.

- **Ketsu (The Conclusion):** Philosophically harmonize the disparate elements of the first two acts with the twist. This does not require a definitive victory or defeat — it is simply an ending that offers a **new, resonant perspective** on the events.

</framework_elements>
</section>

<section name="Framework D: The Fichtean Curve (High-Tension / Thriller)">

**When to use:** The goal is to keep the audience's cortisol levels elevated until the final pages. Thrillers, mysteries, action narratives, and stories that derive their power from relentless momentum.

**Source:** Formalized as an alternative to Freytag's Pyramid (Sources 28, 29, 30)

<framework_elements>

- **In Media Res:** Skip formal exposition entirely. Plunge the reader immediately into a state of crisis. The story begins in the middle of action, danger, or urgency.

- **Escalating Crises:** Construct roughly two-thirds of the story as a continuous, escalating series of crises that constitute a massive rising action. Each crisis must be more severe than the last. The reader must never feel the pressure decrease.

- **Dynamic Backstory Integration:** Weave essential backstory and worldbuilding dynamically into the characters' reactions to these crises. **Never halt momentum for an information dump.** Backstory is revealed through:
  - Active dialogue under pressure
  - Flashbacks triggered by crisis moments
  - Character reactions that reveal history through behavior

- **Sharp, Late-Stage Climax:** The Fichtean Curve culminates in a climax positioned very late in the narrative — the cortisol release must come only at the end.

- **Brief Resolution:** A very short resolution follows. Do not linger. The audience's emotional release is the payoff; extended denouement dissipates it.

</framework_elements>

<contrast_note>
**Why not Freytag's Pyramid?** Formulated in 1863, Freytag's model places the primary climax near the midpoint, leaving a disproportionately long falling action that bleeds the narrative of tension and predictably leads to audience disengagement (Source 28). The Fichtean Curve was specifically designed to address this structural weakness.
</contrast_note>

</section>
