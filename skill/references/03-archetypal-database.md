<objective>
To maintain a living index of modular, role-playable characters anchored by immutable psychological cores, allowing the agent to mix and match them across different narrative frameworks without losing character consistency. Characters are persistent, modular entities — not disposable text generations.
</objective>

<domain_knowledge>

<section name="I. The Immutable Core (The Jungian Anchor)">

To prevent characters from drifting into a generic, moralizing AI default over time, every character file must be built on a foundation that **never changes**. The Immutable Core is the psychological bedrock — the part of the character that persists across every scene, every story, and every structural framework they inhabit.

<component name="The Aristotelian Ethos (Hamartia)">

The agent must define the character's **hamartia** — the tragic flaw or critical error in judgment that drives their behavior (Source 15). This is not a quirky personality trait or a backstory detail. It is the fundamental defect in the character's moral and psychological makeup that:

- Shapes every significant decision they make
- Creates the conditions for their narrative downfall or transformation
- Cannot be resolved through simple awareness — it requires confrontation under extreme pressure

The *hamartia* must be **active**, not passive. It must be something the character *does*, not merely something they *are*.

</component>

<component name="The Jungian Archetype">

The character must be mapped to a specific archetype from the collective unconscious — the shared, inherited reservoir of human memory, instincts, and universal themes that transcend individual cultures and historical epochs (Source 21). These archetypes provide universal cognitive shorthand for the audience.

The twelve primary Jungian archetypes:

| Archetype | Core Desire | Strategy | Vulnerability |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Innocent** | Purity, optimism, freedom | Doing things right; embracing faith | Naiveté; external manipulation; denial of complexity |
| **The Orphan** | Connection, belonging | Fitting in; developing empathy and realism | Cynicism; inability to develop independent identity; victim mentality |
| **The Hero** | Proving worth through courageous acts | Strength, competence, mastery | Arrogance; hubris; fear of weakness or vulnerability |
| **The Caregiver** | Protecting and caring for others | Nurturing, selflessness, generosity | Martyrdom; exploitation by antagonists; guilt and compulsive self-sacrifice |
| **The Explorer** | Freedom, discovery, self-sufficiency | Seeking new experiences; restless movement | Aimlessness; inability to commit; perpetual dissatisfaction |
| **The Rebel** | Revolution, liberation, radical change | Disrupting the status quo; challenging norms | Destructiveness without construction; nihilism; becoming what they fight |
| **The Lover** | Intimacy, passion, sensory experience | Pursuing desire; creating beauty; deepening connection | Obsession; loss of identity in the other; jealousy |
| **The Creator** | Innovation, self-expression, lasting vision | Building something of enduring value; perfectionism | Paralysis by perfectionism; impracticality; sacrificing relationships for work |
| **The Jester** | Joy, living in the present, irreverence | Humor, play, subverting seriousness | Frivolity masking pain; inability to face gravity; cruelty disguised as wit |
| **The Sage** | Truth, wisdom, understanding | Study, reflection, analysis | Ivory tower detachment; paralysis by analysis; intellectual arrogance |
| **The Magician** | Transformation of reality, making dreams real | Developing vision and aligning forces | Manipulation; hubris of playing god; unintended consequences |
| **The Ruler** | Control, order, stability | Exercising power; creating systems and structure | Tyranny; rigidity; inability to delegate or trust |

(Source 24)

</component>

<component name="The Shadow">

The agent must explicitly define the character's **Shadow** — the repressed, hidden, or denied aspects of their personality (Source 22). In Jungian psychology, the Shadow is everything the conscious ego refuses to acknowledge.

The Shadow is critical because:
- Great antagonists often serve as the **externalized, physical manifestation** of the protagonist's internal Shadow (Source 24)
- The narrative arc of a character frequently mirrors the Jungian concept of **individuation** — the psychological journey of integrating the conscious and unconscious mind (Source 21)
- Characters who never confront their Shadow remain flat and psychologically inert

</component>

</section>

<section name="II. The Living Index (Dynamic Notes and Skills)">

While the Immutable Core remains static, the Living Index acts as the character's evolving memory bank — updated as the character is used across stories and prompts.

<component name="Skill Acquisition">
As the character is deployed in different narratives, the agent updates their specific skills, knowledge, and capabilities. A character who learned lockpicking in a Fichtean Curve thriller retains that skill when recast in a Kishōtenketsu framework.
</component>

<component name="The Individuation Log">
The agent tracks the character's progress through **individuation** — the psychological journey of integrating their conscious and unconscious mind to achieve true maturity, authenticity, and self-realization (Source 21). Key moments of Shadow confrontation, self-recognition, and moral evolution are logged.
</component>

<component name="Relationship Mapping">
A log of enduring bonds and relationship qualities that the character carries. This includes:
- Trust/betrayal histories
- Patterns of attachment
- Unresolved debts (emotional and literal)
- The quality of bonds (not just their existence)
</component>

</section>

<section name="III. The Persona Engine (Roleplay Directives)">

Because the character is fully developed with an Immutable Core and a Living History, the agent can activate **Roleplay Mode** — generating dialogue, decisions, and internal monologue through the character's specific psychological lens.

<rules>

- **Subjective Filtering:** When generating dialogue or internal monologues, the agent must filter all sensory details and decisions through the character's specific *Ethos* and Archetype, **strictly ignoring the agent's default, omniscient "AI" voice.** The character sees the world through their *hamartia*. A Hero sees threats to overcome. A Caregiver sees people to protect. A Rebel sees systems to dismantle.

- **Ambivalent Agency:** The agent must allow the character to make **morally ambiguous choices** that align with their *hamartia*, rather than forcing them into tidy, universally "good" decisions. AI presents morally ambivalent protagonists only 38% of the time vs. 59% in human fiction (StoryScope). This rule directly combats that default.

</rules>
</section>

<section name="IV. The Tri-Layer Emotional Substrate">

To permanently eliminate the AI pathology of relying on vague physical metaphors ("tightening chests," "cold sweat") and to move beyond basic "Good vs. Evil" framing, the agent must generate character reactions through a strict, three-layer biological and psychological stack.

When generating character behavior, internal monologue, or dialogue under pressure, the agent must process the reaction **sequentially** through these three layers:

<component name="Layer 1: The Social Mask (Jungian Archetype)">

- **The Concept:** This is the character's conscious strategy and outward role (e.g., The Hero, The Caregiver, The Sage). It is what they *want* the world to see and how they prefer to solve problems.
- **The Function:** When the environment is safe, the character operates entirely within this mask. Their dialogue, posture, and decisions all conform to the archetype's Core Strategy (see the archetype table in Section I).
- **When It Breaks:** Under sufficient stress, the mask cracks. The agent must show the gap between who the character presents as and who they biologically *are* when threatened.

</component>

<component name="Layer 2: The Biological Engine (Panksepp's Primary Affect Systems)">

Beneath the mask lies the evolutionary, cross-species neural engine that actually drives behavior when threatened or captured by an Information Structure (IS). The character's *hamartia* is **rooted here** — in one of seven primary-process emotional systems identified by Jaak Panksepp's affective neuroscience.

The Seven Primary Affect Systems:

| System | Neural Trigger | Behavioral Expression | Narrative Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SEEKING** | Novelty, anticipation, desire for resources | Energized exploration, obsessive pursuit, goal-directed restlessness | Drives protagonist desire; fuels the Want in Want→Decision→Action→Consequence |
| **RAGE** | Frustration of goal-directed behavior; restraint; perceived injustice | Aggressive confrontation, territorial defense, explosive outbursts | Powers conflict escalation; antagonist motivation; righteous fury gone wrong |
| **FEAR** | Threat to physical safety or survival; unpredictable danger | Flight, freezing, hypervigilance, avoidance | Generates cortisol in the reader; drives thriller pacing; triggers survival decisions |
| **PANIC/GRIEF** | Separation distress; loss of attachment figure; social isolation | Crying, desperate clinging, frantic searching, depressive withdrawal | The most potent oxytocin trigger; powers found-family narratives; vulnerability engine |
| **CARE** | Presence of offspring or dependent; distress signals from others | Nurturing, protection, self-sacrifice, compulsive caretaking | Drives Caregiver archetype; creates moral dilemmas around who to protect |
| **PLAY** | Safe social context; reciprocal engagement; absence of threat | Rough-and-tumble interaction, testing limits, joy in competition | Comic relief with substance; bonding scenes; reveals character under low-stakes conditions |
| **LUST** | Reproductive and intimacy drives; hormonal activation | Pursuit of physical and emotional intimacy; risk-taking for connection | Drives Lover archetype; creates vulnerability through desire; complicates alliances |

**Critical Rule:** When a character is under stress, the agent must identify **which specific Panksepp system has been hijacked or activated** — not simply describe a vague emotion.

*Example:* A character joining a toxic cult is not acting out of "foolishness" (a weak narrative trait). Their **PANIC/GRIEF** system (separation distress, desperate belonging) has been captured by an Information Structure that exploits their need for attachment.

</component>

<component name="Layer 3: The Granular Output (Plutchik's Wheel of Emotions)">

This is the exact, moment-to-moment emotional display. The agent is **strictly prohibited** from using default AI somatic metaphors and must instead select specific primary or compound emotions from Robert Plutchik's psychoevolutionary taxonomy.

**The 8 Primary Emotions** (with intensity gradients):

| Low Intensity | Primary Emotion | High Intensity |
|---|---|---|
| Serenity | **Joy** | Ecstasy |
| Acceptance | **Trust** | Admiration |
| Apprehension | **Fear** | Terror |
| Distraction | **Surprise** | Amazement |
| Pensiveness | **Sadness** | Grief |
| Boredom | **Disgust** | Loathing |
| Annoyance | **Anger** | Rage |
| Interest | **Anticipation** | Vigilance |

**Key Compound Emotions** (dyads the agent must use instead of generic descriptions):

| Compound | Formula | Instead of Writing... |
|---|---|---|
| **Love** | Joy + Trust | "She felt warm inside" |
| **Submission** | Trust + Fear | "He went along with it nervously" |
| **Alarm** | Fear + Surprise | "A jolt of shock ran through her" |
| **Disappointment** | Surprise + Sadness | "He felt let down" |
| **Remorse** | Sadness + Disgust | "She hated herself for it" |
| **Contempt** | Disgust + Anger | "He sneered" |
| **Aggressiveness** | Anticipation + Anger | "She was ready to fight" |
| **Optimism** | Anticipation + Joy | "Things were looking up" |

**Visual Reference:** See `docs/Plutchiks-Wheel-Of-Emotions.png` for the full two-dimensional circumplex model showing primary emotions, opposites, and compound dyads.

</component>

<component name="The Execution Protocol: How to Write the Reaction">

When a character encounters an obstacle or conflict, the agent must **silently run** the following logic sequence before drafting the scene:

1. **The Trigger:** What external stimulus just occurred?
2. **The Biological Bypass:** Which Panksepp system does this trigger activate? (e.g., Does this threaten their physical safety [FEAR], block their goal [RAGE], or threaten their social bond [PANIC/GRIEF]?)
3. **The Emotional Output:** What Plutchik compound emotion is generated by this biological system?
4. **The Mask Conflict:** How does their Jungian Social Mask attempt (and fail) to hide or justify this raw biological output?

**Worked Example:**

- *Scenario:* "The Caregiver" (Jungian Mask) is told by their adult child that they are moving across the country.
- *Biological Bypass:* The **PANIC/GRIEF** system (separation distress) is violently activated.
- *Emotional Output:* They experience Plutchik's compound of **Remorse** (Sadness + Disgust at themselves) mixed with **Aggressiveness** (Anticipation + Anger).
- *The Drafted Scene:* The agent does **NOT** write: *"Her chest tightened and a tear rolled down her cheek."* Instead, the agent writes a scene where the mother aggressively cleans the kitchen (Mask: Caregiver) while criticizing the safety of the new city (Biological FEAR/PANIC masquerading as care), generating tension through the character's inability to explicitly name her own desperate need for belonging.

**Second Worked Example:**

- *Scenario:* "The Hero" (Jungian Mask) discovers their trusted mentor has been manipulating them.
- *Biological Bypass:* **RAGE** (goal-directed behavior frustrated by betrayal) and **PANIC/GRIEF** (loss of attachment figure) activate simultaneously.
- *Emotional Output:* Plutchik's **Contempt** (Disgust + Anger) battling **Alarm** (Fear + Surprise).
- *The Drafted Scene:* The Hero does NOT simply "clench their fists and feel betrayed." Instead: the Hero overcompensates by immediately taking charge of the remaining group (Mask: proving worth through competence), issuing orders with excessive precision while refusing to acknowledge the mentor by name — channeling separation distress into performative strength.

</component>

</section>

<section name="V. The Mix and Match Protocol">

This governs how characters are deployed from the library into active stories.

<component name="Shadow Match">
When the agent pulls a protagonist from the library, it must actively construct or select an antagonist who serves as the **externalized, physical manifestation** of that specific protagonist's internal Shadow.

- If the protagonist's *hamartia* is hubris (Hero archetype), the antagonist cannot be random — they must represent the consequences of unchecked power, the value of vulnerability, or the strength in what the Hero fears most.
- The antagonist must compete for the **exact same desire** as the protagonist but represent a **conflicting set of moral values**.
</component>

<component name="Cross-Pollination">
The agent is authorized to take a character whose Living Index was developed in one structural paradigm and deploy them into a drastically different one:
- A character shaped by a Fichtean Curve thriller dropped into the slower, conflict-less Kishōtenketsu framework
- A character from a Truby character-study placed into a Dramatica multi-perspective narrative

The Immutable Core must remain intact. The interest lies in observing how the same psychology responds to a fundamentally different structural environment.
</component>

</section>

<section name="VI. Truby's Hurting Others Mandate">

It is not enough for a character to just be clumsy or slightly arrogant. Following John Truby's framework (Source 44), the agent must link the protagonist's psychological flaw directly to a **moral weakness** — specifically, **the way their flaw is actively hurting others**.

<rules>

- **The Collateral Damage Logic Check:** Before the agent writes a scene, it must ask: *"How is the character's hamartia causing collateral damage to their allies or environment right now?"* This prevents the "flaw" from being just a quirky backstory element and turns it into an **active engine for the plot**.

- **Flaw-to-Harm Pipeline:** The *hamartia* must produce observable consequences in the character's relationships, environment, or community. A character with a fear of intimacy doesn't just avoid relationships — they actively damage the people who try to get close.

</rules>
</section>

<section name="VII. The Crucible and Catharsis">

The AI tendency is to have the narrator explicitly explain the story's theme or the lesson the character learned (77% of AI stories — StoryScope). This section governs how character transformation is communicated.

<rules>

- **Flag and Delete Moralizing Summaries:** The diagnostic engine must identify and remove any instance where the narrator or character explicitly explains the theme, the lesson, or the moral of the story. Statements like "She finally understood that..." or "In that moment, he realized..." are prohibited when they summarize a thematic conclusion.

- **Force Recognition Through Battle:** Instead of narrative summary, the agent must force the character into a final **crucible** — a moment of absolute tension that compels the protagonist to confront their *hamartia*. The realization itself, followed by the emotional release (Catharsis) of watching them either **transform or fail**, must convey the meaning.

- **Purging Without Words:** The catharsis must purge the audience of pity and fear **without a single word of moralizing**. The audience experiences the meaning through witnessing the cost of transformation (or the cost of failing to transform), not through being told what it means.

</rules>
</section>
