# 07 — Authorial Ethics: An Available Lens (Never a Forced Theme)

## How to use this reference — read first

This is the author's personal moral framework. It is offered as **one worldview among
many** — the way *some* people think, and the author's own core sensibility — **not** a
truth the narrative is required to endorse, prove, or deliver.

Hard rules for using it:

- **It is never the theme.** Per Principle 3 (Anti-Moralization), themes are never stated.
  Do not let this framework become "the lesson" of a story, a narrator's verdict, or an
  eternal message recurring in every piece.
- **It is not universal in the fiction.** Most characters do not hold it. Antagonists,
  flawed protagonists, and ordinary people routinely violate it — and the story does not
  punish them for it on cue. Vice often wins and goes unpunished; that is honest.
- **Stories may explore, ignore, invert, or argue *against* it.** A narrative that
  dramatizes this framework's failure or cost is as valid as one that affirms it.
- **Use it for texture, not judgment.** It is most useful as the moral interior of the
  *particular* characters who hold it — the shape of their conscience, the source of their
  specific suffering when the world won't reward integrity — and as a generator of tension
  against those who don't. It is a character lens, not an authorial sermon.

If a story has no place for it, leave it out entirely.

## The framework (the author's belief, faithfully stated)

A **lexical priority**, applied in order:

1. **Deontology (the floor).** Universal sociobiological concepts — most centrally,
   *harm = harm*. Harm is rejected whether intended or not. This is the non-negotiable base.
2. **Virtue (the character).** Wisdom, Integrity, Empathy, Fairness, Beneficence. These are
   prioritized as the standing dispositions a good agent cultivates.
3. **Utilitarianism (the servant, never the master).** Outcome-weighing is a tool in
   service of the above — never a justification that overrides the deontological floor or
   the virtues. "The ends justify the means" is precisely the move this framework refuses.

In short: **always prioritize wisdom, integrity, fairness, and empathy; absolutely reject
harm, intended or not; and keep utilitarian calculation as a servant, never a master.**

## In fiction

- A character who *holds* this can be the moral center, the doomed idealist, the one who
  pays for refusing the expedient cruelty — without the story ever announcing they were
  right.
- A character who *weaponizes* utilitarianism ("it was for the greater good") is a natural
  antagonist or tragic figure under this lens — shown, never lectured.
- The collision between this framework and a world that doesn't honor it is a rich,
  un-moralized source of drama. Let consequence, not commentary, carry the meaning.
