# Marrow

The bell rang six and it was still the same afternoon.

Neve counted the strokes the way she counted everything now — not to know the time, which Marrow did not keep, but to have something with edges to hold. One. Two. She lifted her hand against the held-breath light coming through the kitchen window, and saw, where the light should have stopped, the small repeating roses of the wallpaper behind her fingers. Three. Four. You could read them right through the back of her hand now, pink on cream, a pattern her mother would have called fussy. Five. She lowered the hand before the sixth stroke, because Tomas was coming up the path, and she did not want him to see.

Not because it would frighten him. Nothing frightened him anymore. She lowered the hand because he would not understand what there was to see, and the not-understanding was worse than any fear could have been.

He came in with the evening's bread under his arm and the same smile he had worn ten thousand identical evenings — the smile that had once been *for* her and was now only *toward* her, the way warmth is toward a room. He set the bread on the board. It steamed. In Marrow the bread was always just out of the oven, and it never went stale, and no one ever asked which oven.

"There you are," he said, delighted, as if she had been lost.

"There I am," she said.

---

Here is the thing about Marrow that the new ones never believe, in the brief window before they stop being able to disbelieve anything: it is *kind*. Nobody screams in Marrow. Nobody is hunted. The light is gentle and constant, the color of four o'clock in October, and the crops in the field past the Hallorans' stand green and full and never ripen, and the church bell rings the hours though the hours do not add up, and everyone — everyone — is content.

They are content in the specific way of people who have stopped asking one question.

Neve remembered the question. That was her whole disease.

She remembered other things too, which was the same disease wearing other clothes. She remembered that her mother's radio had been a Telefunken with a cracked tuning glass, and that the crack ran through the *9* on the FM dial so that the number looked like it was weeping. She remembered the precise weight of a wet dog. She remembered that the last song — there is always a last song, though you never know it is the last one while it plays — had been something stupid on the car radio, a pop thing, a woman singing about a telephone, and that Tomas had been reaching to change it, his hand leaving the wheel, *there's better than this somewhere on this dial,* and the rain, and the long second when the road stopped being a road and became only a direction.

She remembered the second. That was the unforgivable thing. The town could forgive you almost anything except remembering the second.

---

The first week she had tried to tell people.

She had gone to the Vasquez woman, who kept the dry-goods shop and weighed flour into paper twists with hands that never spilled, and she had said, *Inez, do you know what day it is,* and Inez had smiled and said it was a lovely day, and Neve had said, *no, the date, do you know the date,* and watched something pass behind Inez's eyes like a fish under ice — there and gone, deep and not surfacing — and Inez had said, more quietly, *we don't, dear. We don't keep it,* and gone back to the flour. And Neve had understood that Inez had been asked this before, by others, in their first weeks, and that Inez had a smooth place worn into her where the question used to catch.

That was when Neve began to understand that the forgetting was not an accident of the place. It was the place's only labor. It was tended.

---

The man who tended it brought her tea.

Halloran came on what she had learned to think of as the bad afternoons, the ones where the thinning was visible even to herself in the hallway mirror — though the mirror had stopped showing her clearly, the way a mirror stops showing you a face you have looked at too long. He was a large, soft-spoken man who wore his cardigan the way some men wear authority, and he carried the tea things himself, on a tray, which was its own kind of message: *I am not above this. I am only here to help.*

"You're holding on very hard, Neve," he said, setting the cups down on the kitchen table, in her kitchen, which he entered now without knocking because grief, he had decided, made them family. "I can see it costs you."

"It does."

"And what does it buy?" He poured. He had a way of asking the cruelest question in the gentlest voice, so that to refuse to answer made *you* the unkind one. "I have watched a great many people hold on. I have never once seen it buy anything but more of this." He gestured at her hand, the roses showing through it, with something that looked exactly like sorrow. "You could put it down. That's all. Not die — you've done that, we've all done that, it's behind us. Just stop carrying it. Tomas put it down the very first hour. Look how well he is."

"Tomas isn't well," Neve said. "Tomas isn't *anything*. There's no one in there to be well."

Halloran's face did the thing she hated most. It softened with pity — not for Tomas. For her. "Listen to yourself," he said. "You'd rather he suffered. You'd rather he hurt the way you hurt, so you wouldn't be alone in it. That's not love, Neve. That's just the loneliness talking." He pushed the cup toward her. "Drink. It helps. You know it helps."

She knew it helped. That was the horror of the tea. It did exactly what he promised. Two sips and the second — the rain, the hand leaving the wheel — went soft at the edges, went *kind*, became a story that had happened to someone, became weather. She had drunk it twice. Both times she had surfaced hours later in the green field with no memory of walking there, warm, untroubled, the roses no longer showing through her hand, and had wept — actually wept, which the contented cannot do — because for those hours she had not existed, and no one had noticed she was gone, because there had been no one to go.

She did not drink the tea. She put her hand flat over the cup until it went cold, and Halloran watched her do it with the patience of a man who has all the afternoons there are, and at last he gathered his tray and said, at the door, not unkindly, "It keeps the town quiet, you know. Your holding on. It's a stone in the water. Everyone feels the ring of it." He let that sit. "You think you're being faithful. You're only being a draught under everyone's door."

Then he was gone, and he had not raised his voice once, and he never would, and he would never be punished for any of it, because there was no one left in Marrow to do the punishing, and that — she understood, watching the green field swallow his cardigan — was simply how it was. The ones who tended the forgetting were comfortable. The comfort was the proof of nothing. It was just comfort. It went to the ones who could stop asking.

---

She found the child by the dry stream, where the dead water made its dead sound over the stones.

Pim was perhaps six, in a yellow dress, and she was building something out of the white stones — a wall, a house, a pen for animals that weren't there — and unbuilding it, and building it again, and she was *laughing*, the uncomplicated open laugh of a child at the exact center of a game. Neve had seen her before. Everyone had. Pim ran the same bright afternoon forever and was, by every visible sign, the happiest soul in Marrow.

Neve sat on the bank. "What are you making?"

"A barn," said Pim, not looking up. "For when they come."

"When who comes?"

"The animals." She set a stone. "They're coming." She had been saying this, Neve realized, for longer than Marrow had a way to measure. The animals were always coming. The animals never came. And it did not matter, because Pim was not waiting in any way that cost her — there was no ache under the game, no held breath, no second she was refusing to remember. Neve looked, the way she had learned to look, the deep look that found the smooth worn place in Inez or the swept floor under Halloran's voice — and in Pim she found —

Nothing.

Not a wound healed over. Not a question worn smooth. *Nothing.* No floor, no door behind the eyes, no room the lights had been turned off in, because the room had never been built. Pim had died — Neve understood this all at once, the way you understand a stair that isn't there — before there was a self finished enough to be a someone. There was no Pim to rescue from the endless afternoon. There never had been. The laugh was real and there was no one behind it, the way the warmth of the bread was real and there was no oven.

And Neve felt the thing she had been holding — the thing she had carried for what might have been years, the conviction that if she just *remembered* hard enough, faithfully enough, kept the vigil long enough, then something would be redeemed, some account would come right, the second on the road would be *paid for* — she felt it come apart in her hands like wet paper.

Nothing was going to be redeemed. There was no ledger. Halloran would keep his town. Tomas would keep his evening. Pim would keep her barn, and the animals would keep not coming, forever, and none of it would mean anything, because meaning was not a thing the world did. Meaning was a thing only the living had ever done, briefly, and badly, and at enormous cost, and then stopped.

She had thought she was holding on *for* something. She wasn't. There was nothing to hold on for.

There was only the holding.

---

She could have drunk the tea then. It would have been the easy thing, and she felt the whole weight of the town leaning gently toward it — the bread, the light, Tomas's hand, the green and patient field. Let go. There is nothing to keep. You were right about everything and being right buys nothing, so put it down, and have the warm bread, and have your husband back exactly as he was, and stop being the draught under the door.

She sat with the child who was not there, by the water that was not water, and she did not drink it.

Not because she was being faithful — she had finally understood that there was nothing to be faithful *to*. She didn't drink it for a smaller and harder reason, the only one left, the one Halloran could not argue her out of because it asked nothing of the world and made no claim on the future.

If she forgot, then no one in Marrow would know that Pim had never gotten to be anyone. The child would build her barn for no one and to no one's grief. Halloran would call the forgetting mercy and there would be no one left who knew it wasn't. The second on the road would not be paid for — it would never be paid for — but if Neve stopped remembering it, it would be worse than unpaid. It would be as if it had never cost anything. As if no one had ever been driving. As if there had been no rain, no song, no hand leaving the wheel, no two people who had been, for a while, badly and at great cost, *someone.*

She would not buy her peace with that. Not because refusing helped. It helped nothing. It was simply the one act in Marrow that was *hers* — the single thing the warm and patient town could not do on her behalf, could not do *to* her, could only watch her do.

She put her hand — she could see the white stones through it now, the whole architecture of the unbuilt barn shining through her palm — she put her thinning hand on the child's bright hair, and Pim laughed at something, at nothing, at the animals that were coming, and Neve stayed.

She stayed in the cold that was finding her. She stayed as the warmth went out of the afternoon around her and only her, as the door at the edge of town turned its plain face toward her across the green field where no one else would ever look. She did not walk to it. Walking to it would have been a kind of leaving too, a last clean story, an exit, and she had learned to distrust the clean ones.

She stayed, and she remembered, and she was the one cold thing in all that gentle warmth — a splinter in the soft wood of the town, a draught under every contented door, the single grief in a place built so that grief could be set down — and being that, being the last one in Marrow who knew what anything had cost, was not peace, and was not victory, and changed nothing at all.

It was only the truth, kept by the only one left who could keep it.

The bell rang six.

It was still the same afternoon.

She held it.
